# People & Culture Presentations

Human-first decks that inform, inspire, and connect — for HR, People Ops, and leadership teams. People decks balance professionalism with warmth and prioritize clarity over density. They tell stories, celebrate humans, and communicate with empathy.

## Tone

Warm, inclusive, human-first, and professional. Avoid cold or overly clinical language. Use "we" and "our team" rather than "the organization" or "human capital." Active voice. Positive framing where genuine — don't spin bad news, but acknowledge it humanely. When discussing challenges (turnover, attrition, DEI gaps), lead with empathy, then facts, then action.

Avoid: corporate jargon, passive voice, gendered language, "resources" when referring to people. Use: "team members", "colleagues", "our people".

## Design

Use the Corporate design system (see `../shared/design-system.md`). People decks are the one corporate subdomain where you may use slightly more white space and fewer data-dense layouts. Visuals and quotes carry more weight than tables or charts. Stakeholder Quote (Pattern 10) is especially powerful here — use it for employee voices and leadership statements.

## Pattern Priorities

1. **Stakeholder Quote** (Pattern 10) — for employee testimonials, culture statements, leadership messages — this pattern is uniquely powerful for people decks
2. **3-Column Strategic Pillars** (Pattern 4) — for values, principles, culture pillars, onboarding framework
3. **Process Steps** (Pattern 9) — for onboarding journeys, career pathways, hiring process
4. **Executive Cover** (Pattern 1) — for all-hands, culture day, major announcements
5. **KPI Scorecard** (Pattern 2) — for engagement scores, DEI metrics, hiring pipeline
6. **Status Dashboard** (Pattern 8) — for DEI program tracking, initiative progress
7. **Dark Stat Band + Context** (Pattern 3) — for engagement survey results, company milestones
8. **Action Items** (Pattern 12) — for commitments, People team actions, follow-ups

## Content Rules

- **People-first language throughout**: "We hired 240 talented people" not "headcount increased by 240". "Team members who left" not "attrition." Language signals how the org thinks about its people.
- **Represent diversity in examples**: Names, roles, and hypothetical examples should reflect diverse representation. Use a mix of names from different backgrounds. Never default to a single demographic in all examples.
- **Less data-heavy than other corporate subdomains**: A people deck with 10 KPI cards feels cold. Use 2–3 key metrics per slide and give each one a human context sentence.
- **Visual storytelling preferred**: If you have a choice between a table and a visual narrative, choose the narrative. Stats are more powerful when paired with a story.
- **DEI data requires context**: Never show a DEI gap without (a) acknowledging the gap honestly, (b) explaining what's being done, (c) setting a target with a timeline. Raw data without context can feel tone-deaf.
- **Celebrate specifically, not generically**: "Q1 Award Winner: Priya Chen, Customer Success — for redesigning onboarding documentation that cut time-to-first-value by 40%" is far better than "recognizing our team."
- **Employee quotes must be real-feeling**: Use first-person voice, specific details, and natural language — not formal, polished corporate statements. Quotes should sound like a real person said them.
- **Onboarding decks**: Must be welcoming, practical, and not overwhelming. Prioritize: who we are, how we work, who to talk to, where to find things. 10 slides max for day-1 onboarding.
- **Use action verbs for commitments**: "We will publish pay bands by Q3 2026" not "We are committed to exploring pay transparency."

## Slide-by-Slide Guidance

### Culture Presentation (8–12 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — company name, "Our Culture" title, date
2. Who We Are (Pattern 3: Dark Stat Band) — founding year, team size, locations, mission in one line
3. Our Values (Pattern 4: 3-Column Pillars) — 3 core values, each with definition and a behavioral example
4. What It's Like to Work Here (Pattern 10: Quote) — employee testimonial
5. How We Work (Pattern 9: Process Steps) — meeting cadence, collaboration norms, communication tools
6. Growth & Development (Pattern 4: 3-Column Pillars) — learning budget, career paths, promotion philosophy
7. Benefits & Perks (Pattern 2: KPI Scorecard) — 4 key benefits with icons and brief descriptions
8. DEI Commitments (Pattern 8: Status Dashboard) — 4–5 DEI initiatives with progress and owners
9. Team Quote (Pattern 10) — another employee voice
10. How to Get Involved (Pattern 9: Process Steps) — ERGs, volunteer opportunities, team rituals

### DEI Update (8–10 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — "Diversity, Equity & Inclusion Update", quarter/year
2. Our Commitment (Pattern 10: Quote) — CEO or CHRO statement
3. Where We Are Today (Pattern 2: KPI Scorecard) — 4 representation metrics with trend
4. Hiring Funnel by Demographic (Pattern 7: Bar Chart) — pipeline representation
5. Program Status (Pattern 8: Status Dashboard) — DEI initiatives with RAG
6. Listening & Feedback (Pattern 3: Dark Stat Band) — engagement survey scores, response rate
7. Goals & Commitments (Pattern 6: Roadmap) — Q1–Q4 milestones
8. Action Items (Pattern 12) — specific, owned, dated commitments

### Onboarding Deck (8–10 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — "Welcome to [Company]", new hire name
2. Our Story (Pattern 3: Dark Stat Band) — founding, mission, key milestones
3. Our Values (Pattern 4: 3-Column Pillars) — 3 values with behavioral examples
4. Your First 30/60/90 Days (Pattern 9: Process Steps) — milestone-based onboarding path
5. How We Work (Pattern 4: 3-Column Pillars) — communication norms, meeting culture, async tools
6. Your Team (Pattern 4: 3-Column Pillars) — org structure, direct colleagues, who to ask for what
7. Benefits Overview (Pattern 2: KPI Scorecard) — 4 key benefits
8. Resources & Links (Pattern 12 format — table with resource, link/owner, when to use)
9. Welcome Quote (Pattern 10) — a team member's welcome message to new hires
