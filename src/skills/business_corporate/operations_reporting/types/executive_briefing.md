# Executive Briefing

**What it is:** A concise, high-stakes presentation to senior executives on a specific topic — designed to inform a decision, update on a critical situation, or frame a strategic question in a short time window.

---

## Audience
C-suite (CEO, CFO, COO, board member) in a 15–30 minute slot. Time-constrained, high-context. They'll read the conclusion first. They want: what's happening, what it means, and what you need from them.

## Tone
Crisp, conclusive, and confident. An executive briefing that makes the audience work to find the point has failed. Lead with the conclusion. Defend it with data. Keep every slide to one point.

---

## Slide Structure

1. **Cover** — Topic name, presenter, date, time allotted
2. **The Bottom Line** — Your conclusion in 3 bullet points. What you know, what it means, what you recommend.
3. **Context** — Minimum context needed to understand the situation. No history lesson.
4. **The Situation** — What's happening. Current state in specific, factual terms.
5. **Analysis / Key Findings** — 3 evidence-backed points. The "why" behind the situation.
6. **Implications** — What this means for the business if no action is taken.
7. **Options** — 2–3 response options with trade-offs. No more.
8. **Recommendation** — Your recommended option with rationale.
9. **What You Need** — Specific ask from the executive: decision, resource, escalation, approval.
10. **Appendix** — Supporting data, full analysis, assumptions (leave-behind only).

---

## Must-Have Elements
- Bottom line up front — don't make executives read to slide 8 to find the conclusion
- A recommendation — not just options
- An explicit ask — what you need from the audience
- One point per slide — no slide should require more than 30 seconds to understand
- Appendix with supporting data (executives may want to drill into specifics after)

## Must-Avoid
- Briefings that build to the conclusion like a mystery novel — bottom line first
- Slides with more than 4 bullet points
- Recommendations presented without trade-offs
- No explicit ask — executives need to know what they're being asked to do
- Slides with titles that don't convey the point (use "headline" titles that state the conclusion)

---

## Key Data Points to Include (vary by topic)
- The core metric or indicator driving the briefing
- Timeline: when does a decision need to be made?
- Financial impact of options (approximate)
- Risk assessment: probability and severity

---

## Example Outline (Security Incident Briefing)

1. Security Incident Briefing — [Date] — CEO + CISO
2. The Bottom Line: Contained Incident, No Data Exfiltration, Disclosure Required
3. Context: Our Security Posture and Monitoring Setup
4. The Incident: What Happened, When, How We Found It
5. Analysis: Root Cause, Scope, Customer Impact Assessment
6. Implications: Regulatory Notification Timeline, Customer Communication
7. Options: Proactive Disclosure vs. Reactive vs. Minimum Required
8. Recommendation: Proactive Disclosure Within 72 Hours
9. What We Need: CEO Approval for Communication Plan + Legal Engagement
10. Appendix: Technical Detail + Communication Drafts
