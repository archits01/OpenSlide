# PR / Communications Strategy

**What it is:** A structured communications plan — typically tied to a major announcement, funding round, or market moment — covering narrative strategy, target outlets, message hierarchy, spokesperson plan, and execution timeline.

---

## Audience
CEO, CMO, and founding team. Audience is approving a high-stakes comms strategy and may be unfamiliar with how PR works. Show them the narrative logic, not just the outlet list.

## Tone
Strategic and clear-eyed about what PR can and can't achieve. Avoid PR agency jargon. Frame the strategy around narrative, not placement — placements are the output of a compelling story, not the strategy itself.

---

## Slide Structure

1. **Title** — Company name, announcement type (e.g. "Series C Announcement — PR Strategy")
2. **The Announcement in Context** — What's happening and why it matters. The business moment.
3. **Our Communications Objective** — What we want audiences to believe/feel/do after the announcement
4. **Audience Mapping** — Who are we communicating to? Investors, customers, recruits, regulators. Priority order.
5. **The Narrative Architecture** — 3-act story: Problem → Solution → Validation. The "why this company, why now" angle.
6. **Message Hierarchy** — Primary message (one sentence). Supporting proof points (3). Third-tier detail.
7. **Target Outlets + Journalists** — Tier 1, 2, 3 media. Named journalists where possible. Rationale for each outlet.
8. **Embargo Strategy** — Who gets the story early, when embargo lifts, syndication plan.
9. **Spokesperson Plan** — Who speaks for the company, on which topics. Preparation requirements.
10. **Timeline** — Pre-brief → embargo → launch day → follow-up coverage window.
11. **Risk + Scenario Planning** — What could go wrong: difficult questions, competitor response, negative angle. Prep responses.
12. **Measurement** — How we define success: tier-1 placements, share of voice, narrative accuracy in coverage.

---

## Must-Have Elements
- A narrative architecture — the 3-act story that makes this news interesting to a journalist
- Message hierarchy: primary message + proof points + detail
- Named target journalists where possible — not just outlet names
- Embargo strategy with clear lift time
- Spokesperson prep plan
- Risk scenarios with draft responses

## Must-Avoid
- PR strategies built around outlet lists without narrative rationale
- Announcing without a clear "why now" angle — journalists need the hook
- Using company-centric language in the primary message ("we're excited to announce")
- Underestimating the follow-up phase — launch day is 30% of the work
- Strategies that treat all media as equal — tier and prioritize

---

## Key Data Points to Include
- Announcement: funding amount, round designation, lead investor (if disclosable)
- Target outlet tiers: # Tier 1 targets, # total targets
- Embargo timeline: when briefs go out, when embargo lifts
- Spokesperson: CEO + one subject-matter expert minimum
- Success metric: # Tier 1 placements, specific narrative elements to be present in coverage

---

## Example Outline (AI Health Startup — Series C)

1. Beam Health Series C ($95M) — Communications Strategy
2. The Moment: Series C + 3 Years of Proving the Model
3. Our Communications Objective: Establish Beam as the Infrastructure Layer for Clinical AI
4. Audiences: Investors, Health Systems, Clinicians, Policy, Talent
5. The Narrative: Clinician Burnout (Problem) → Beam's AI (Solution) → Outcomes Data (Validation)
6. Message Hierarchy: Primary + 3 Proof Points
7. Target Outlets: STAT News, TechCrunch, Health Affairs, WSJ — Named Journalists
8. Embargo Strategy: 72-Hour Brief Window, Exclusive to STAT News
9. Spokesperson Plan: CEO (company story) + CMO (clinical outcomes)
10. Timeline: Day –14 to Day +30
11. Risk Scenarios: "AI Replaces Doctors" Angle, Data Privacy Questions
12. Measurement: 3+ Tier-1 Placements, Narrative Score in Coverage
