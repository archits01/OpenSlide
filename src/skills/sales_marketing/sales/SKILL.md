# Sales Presentations

Persuasive, customer-focused decks built to move deals forward. Sales presentations have one job: advance the buyer's decision. Every slide should either deepen trust, reduce risk, or accelerate urgency. Slides that do none of these should be cut.

## Tone

Persuasive, customer-focused, outcome-driven, and confident. Write from the buyer's perspective — not "Here's what we do" but "Here's your problem, here's how we solve it, here's what your peers achieved, here's what we're asking for." Never defensive. Never list features without connecting them to a buyer outcome. If you can't articulate why the buyer should care, cut it.

Avoid: feature-first framing, jargon, vague claims, defensive competitive positioning ("we're just as good as..."), decks that end without an explicit ask.

Use: buyer outcomes, social proof at every stage, specific numbers, explicit next steps, customer language (use their industry terminology).

## Design

Use the Sales & Marketing design system (see `../shared/design-system.md`). Sales decks are shown in live meetings — they must work at a glance. Prioritize large numbers, bold headlines, and clean layouts over information density. Every slide should be readable in 5 seconds.

The accent color (var(--slide-accent)) must be prominent — it signals confidence and brand identity. Dark panels anchor the deck and add gravitas.

## Narrative Structure (MANDATORY)

Every sales deck MUST follow this sequence:
1. **Pain** — establish the problem with data and empathy
2. **Solution** — introduce the product/service as the answer
3. **Proof** — show that it works (case studies, testimonials, ROI data)
4. **Ask** — make a specific, clear call to action

Never skip steps. Never reorder. Buyers who see proof before understanding the problem don't believe it. Buyers who see the ask before proof don't take it.

## Pattern Priorities

1. **Sales Cover** (Pattern 1) — personalized to prospect, always includes company name
2. **Problem / Pain Point** (Pattern 2) — quantified pain, data-backed urgency
3. **Solution Overview** (Pattern 3) — feature → benefit → outcome structure
4. **Social Proof — Logos + Testimonial** (Pattern 4) — customer logos + strong testimonial
5. **Results / ROI Stats** (Pattern 5) — quantified impact from real customers
6. **Case Study** (Pattern 6) — deep-dive proof relevant to the prospect's industry/size
7. **Competitive Comparison** (Pattern 7) — when asked, always shows us winning
8. **Pricing / Tiers** (Pattern 9) — structured offer with recommendation
9. **Customer Journey / Funnel** (Pattern 10) — for QBRs, showing customer progress
10. **Call to Action** (Pattern 12) — always the last slide, always explicit

## Content Rules

- **Structure MUST follow: pain → solution → proof → ask**: This is not optional. A deck that opens with your company history and then lists features is not a sales deck — it's a brochure. Start with the buyer's pain.
- **Every claim needs a number**: "We help teams be more productive" is not allowed. "We help sales teams generate 41% more revenue per rep within 6 months" is. Numbers are the currency of persuasion in B2B sales.
- **Case studies before pricing**: Never show pricing before at least one case study. Buyers need to understand value before they can evaluate cost. Showing price too early invites sticker shock without context.
- **Always end with an explicit next step**: The last slide must have a specific ask — not "let us know if you have questions" but "Schedule a 30-minute technical review this week" or "Sign the pilot agreement by April 17." Vague closes lose deals.
- **Personalize the cover and the pain slide to the prospect**: The cover must include the prospect's company name. The pain slide must reference their industry or known situation. Generic decks lose deals.
- **The ask should match the buyer's stage**: Discovery → ask for a next meeting. Evaluation → ask for a pilot. Late stage → ask for signature or verbal commit. Don't ask for too much too early.
- **Competitive slides must recommend, not just compare**: If you show a competitive table, the last row or summary must recommend your product unambiguously. Neutral comparisons look weak.
- **ROI stats must show methodology**: "8.2× ROI" requires at minimum: "Average across N customers over M months." Unverifiable claims reduce trust.
- **QBR decks are renewal pitches**: A QBR is not a status update — it's a chance to prove value and advance the renewal. Structure QBRs around: (1) value delivered this period, (2) business outcomes achieved, (3) roadmap for next period, (4) renewal/expansion ask.

## Slide-by-Slide Guidance

### Standard Sales Pitch (10–14 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — personalized: "For [Prospect Company] · April 10, 2026"
2. The Problem (Pattern 2: Pain Point) — quantified pain relevant to their industry
3. Why Now (Pattern 3: Dark Stat Band) — market urgency, timing context, cost of inaction
4. Solution Overview (Pattern 3: Solution) — 3 core capabilities
5. Product Demo (Pattern 8: Screenshot) — 3 annotated UI callouts
6. Customer Logos + Testimonial (Pattern 4: Social Proof) — relevant reference customers
7. Case Study (Pattern 6) — most relevant customer story to prospect's profile
8. ROI Stats (Pattern 5) — quantified impact from customer base
9. Why We Win (Pattern 7: Competitive) — optional, if competitive displacement
10. How It Works (Pattern 9: Process Steps) — onboarding and implementation journey
11. Pricing (Pattern 9: Pricing Tiers) — 3 options, recommended highlighted
12. Next Steps (Pattern 12: CTA) — specific ask with date

### Proposal (8–10 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — "Proposal for [Company]", proposal date, valid-through date
2. Our Understanding (Pattern 2: Problem) — reflect their stated problem back with specifics
3. Recommended Solution (Pattern 3: Solution) — tailored to their needs
4. Implementation Plan (Pattern 9: Process Steps) — timeline specific to their scope
5. Investment (Pattern 9: Pricing) — single recommended option with line-item detail
6. ROI Projection (Pattern 5: Stats) — their specific numbers, not generic averages
7. Why Us (Pattern 4: Social Proof) — relevant references and G2/analyst credentials
8. Next Steps (Pattern 12: CTA) — contract signature date, implementation kickoff date

### Customer QBR (8–10 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — "Q1 2026 Business Review for [Customer]"
2. Executive Summary (Pattern 2: KPI Scorecard from Corporate) — 4 headline metrics vs. their goals
3. Value Delivered (Pattern 5: ROI Stats) — specific ROI metrics for THIS customer
4. Usage & Adoption (Pattern 7: Bar Chart) — usage trend, feature adoption, user counts
5. Progress vs. Goals (Pattern 8: Status Dashboard) — their stated goals with RAG status
6. Customer Voice (Pattern 10: Quote) — testimonial from their team if available
7. What's Next (Pattern 6: Roadmap) — new capabilities relevant to their roadmap
8. Expansion Opportunity (Pattern 9: Pricing) — upsell/cross-sell tied to their goals
9. Action Items &amp; Renewal (Pattern 12: CTA) — renewal date, expansion ask, next meeting

### Competitive Battlecard Deck (6–8 slides)
1. Cover (Pattern 1) — "Competitive Brief: [Competitor Name]"
2. When You'll See Them (Pattern 3: Dark Stat Band) — typical deal context, prospect profile
3. Feature Comparison (Pattern 7: Competitive Table) — our win areas highlighted
4. Objection Handling (Pattern 4: 3-Column Pillars) — top 3 objections and responses
5. Our Win Narrative (Pattern 10: Quote) — customer quote from competitive win
6. Trap-Setting Questions (Pattern 12: Action Items format) — discovery questions that expose competitor weaknesses
