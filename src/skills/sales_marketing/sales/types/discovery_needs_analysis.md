# Discovery / Needs Analysis Deck

**What it is:** A structured presentation for a discovery or qualification call — used to frame the conversation, confirm understanding of the prospect's situation, and ask the right questions to qualify the opportunity and set up a strong demo.

---

## Audience
CISO, VP Engineering, CTO, or senior operational leader in initial stages of a buying process. They've agreed to a discovery call — they want to feel heard, not interrogated.

## Tone
Curious, consultative, and unhurried. Discovery is not a qualification checklist — it's a diagnostic conversation. Show you've done homework. Ask questions that show you understand their world.

---

## Slide Structure

1. **Cover** — Your company name, prospect company, date
2. **About Us: 90 Seconds** — Who you are, what you do. Brief. You're here to listen, not pitch.
3. **Our Understanding of Your Situation** — What you know about them already. Confirm and invite corrections.
4. **What We're Here to Do** — Frame the call: understand their priorities, not sell to them.
5. **The Challenges We Hear in Your Space** — Common pain points in their category. Invite them to react.
6. **Discovery Questions: Current State** — Questions about their environment, tools, team, and process.
7. **Discovery Questions: Pain + Impact** — Questions about what's costing them: time, money, risk, frustration.
8. **Discovery Questions: Decision + Timeline** — Budget authority, decision process, timeline, success criteria.
9. **What Good Looks Like** — Share a benchmark or outcome from a comparable company. Get their reaction.
10. **Relevant Capabilities (brief)** — 2–3 capabilities most relevant to what they've shared. Not a demo.
11. **Proposed Next Steps** — Based on what you've learned, propose a path forward (demo, pilot, RFP response).

---

## Must-Have Elements
- Pre-call research shown on slide 3 — shows you didn't waste their time
- Open-ended pain questions — not yes/no qualification checkboxes
- Budget and decision-process questions asked (budget, process, timeline, stakeholders)
- A "what good looks like" proof point to anchor their thinking
- A proposed next step based on what you learned — not generic

## Must-Avoid
- Discovery calls that are really product pitches in disguise
- Asking the same qualifying questions every prospect gets — customize to their context
- Skipping the "what's your decision process" question — it's the most important
- Ending without a proposed next step
- Slides dense with features — this is a listening call

---

## Key Data Points to Include (to uncover, not present)
- Current tool count and integration complexity
- Number of FTEs managing relevant function
- Volume/scale of the problem (transactions, alerts, incidents per period)
- Budget: does one exist, who owns it, what's the range
- Timeline: when do they need to be live?
- Decision process: who else is involved, what's the evaluation criteria

---

## Example Outline (Enterprise Cybersecurity — CISO Discovery)

1. [Your Company] × [Prospect Company] — Security Discovery Call
2. About Us: The 90-Second Version
3. What We Know About Your Situation (Please Correct Us)
4. What We're Here to Do: Understand Before We Recommend
5. What We Hear from CISOs in Financial Services: Top 3 Pain Points
6. Current State: Your Environment, Stack, and Team
7. Pain + Impact: What's It Costing You? What Are the Risks?
8. Decision + Timeline: How Will You Evaluate? Who's Involved? When?
9. What "Fixed" Looks Like: How a Similar FinServ CISO Solved This
10. What Might Be Relevant to You (2 Capabilities, 2 Minutes)
11. Proposed Path Forward: Full Demo With Your SOC Team on [Date]?
