# Product Demo Presentation

**What it is:** A structured demo session — combining a presentation deck with live product walkthrough — designed to show how the product solves specific pain points for the buyer's situation.

---

## Audience
Operational decision-maker (e.g. Head of Operations, IT Director) at a company actively evaluating solutions. They've agreed to a demo — they want to see themselves using this product.

## Tone
Conversational, discovery-led, specific to this buyer. The demo is not a feature tour — it's a story about their problem and how your product solves it. Reference their specific context throughout.

---

## Slide Structure

1. **Cover** — Product name, company name, meeting date
2. **Agenda** — What we'll cover today. Sets expectations. Invite them to add to it.
3. **Their Situation (in your words)** — Briefly state what you understand about their current state. Confirm before proceeding.
4. **The Problems We'll Solve Today** — 2–3 specific pain points you're addressing in this demo. Name them explicitly.
5. **How [Product] Works: The 30-Second Overview** — Before diving into the product, orient them.
6. **[Live Demo Section 1]** — First use case / pain point. Show the workflow they care about most.
7. **[Live Demo Section 2]** — Second use case. Pause and invite questions between sections.
8. **[Live Demo Section 3]** — Third use case if time allows. Skip if running long.
9. **What You'd Need to Get Started** — Implementation effort, integrations required, timeline to live.
10. **Pricing Overview** — Give them a number or range. Don't make them ask.
11. **What Customers Like You Have Seen** — 1–2 outcomes from comparable companies.
12. **Next Steps** — What happens after this call. Decision process, timeline, stakeholders needed.

---

## Must-Have Elements
- Confirm their pain points before starting the demo — don't assume
- Demo structure driven by their use cases, not product feature order
- Pause for questions between demo sections — not at the end
- Implementation and timeline slide — every buyer worries about disruption
- Pricing before the end — don't make them chase it
- A clear next step with a proposed date

## Must-Avoid
- Showing every feature — show only what solves their stated problems
- Using demo data that looks nothing like their business
- Running through the product without stopping to confirm relevance
- Leaving pricing for a separate call — it signals lack of confidence
- Ending without proposing a specific next step with a date

---

## Key Data Points to Include
- Buyer's current tool count (if known from discovery)
- Current pain in quantifiable terms (hours spent, error rate, cost)
- Implementation timeline: weeks to go live
- Pricing: range or model
- Customer outcomes: at least one comparable company with results
